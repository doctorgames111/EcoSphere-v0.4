Carbon Capture

Systems that remove CO2 directly from the air or industrial sources.

Introduction

Carbon capture has emerged as a cornerstone of global strategies to mitigate climate change. As nations work toward limiting global warming to well below 2 °C — and pursuing efforts toward 1.5 °C — relative to pre‑industrial levels, reducing emissions from fossil fuel use and industrial processes has become urgent. However, even with aggressive deployment of renewable energy and energy efficiency measures, many sectors (such as cement, steel, chemicals, and aviation) will continue to emit carbon dioxide (CO₂) for decades. This is where carbon capture technologies play a vital role.

Carbon capture involves technologies that remove carbon dioxide from large‑point sources or directly from the atmosphere and either store it permanently underground or utilize it in commercial applications. When integrated with carbon storage or utilization, these technologies are often referred to as Carbon Capture, Utilization, and Storage (CCUS) systems. CCUS is widely considered critical to achieving net‑zero emissions by mid‑century while maintaining industrial competitiveness and energy security.

Scientific Background

Carbon dioxide is a naturally occurring component of Earth’s atmosphere and a fundamental part of the global carbon cycle. Through processes such as photosynthesis and respiration, oceans and soils act as sinks that absorb and release carbon. However, the burning of fossil fuels since the Industrial Revolution has increased atmospheric CO₂ concentrations from ~280 parts per million (ppm) in 1750 to over 420 ppm today. This increase strengthens the greenhouse effect, trapping more infrared radiation and driving global warming.

To stabilize the climate, atmospheric CO₂ levels need to be reduced or prevented from rising further. Carbon capture technologies aim to do exactly that — either prevent emissions from entering the atmosphere or remove CO₂ already present.

Carbon Capture Technology Pathways

Carbon capture technologies can be grouped into two broad categories: point‑source capture and direct air capture (DAC).

Point‑Source Capture

This refers to capturing CO₂ at or near the point where it is emitted — such as power plants, refineries, cement factories, or steel mills.

Post‑Combustion Capture

CO₂ is removed from flue gas after fuel has been burned. Chemical solvents (such as amines) selectively bind CO₂, which is then separated and compressed for storage or use.

Pre‑Combustion Capture

Fossil fuels are converted into a synthetic gas (syngas), from which CO₂ is removed before combustion. This method is common in integrated gasification combined cycle (IGCC) power plants.

Oxy‑Fuel Combustion

Fuel is burned in pure oxygen rather than air, producing a flue gas dominated by CO₂ and water vapor, which makes CO₂ easier to separate.

Direct Air Capture (DAC)

Instead of capturing CO₂ at large emission sources, DAC removes CO₂ directly from ambient air. Since atmospheric CO₂ concentrations are much lower than in flue gas, DAC is technically more challenging and energy‑intensive. However, it is uniquely suited for carbon removal — a necessary component of scenarios that aim for net‑zero or net‑negative emissions.

DAC systems use chemical sorbents (solid or liquid) that bind CO₂ from air. Once captured, CO₂ is released in concentrated form through temperature or pressure swings.

What Happens After Capture?

After CO₂ is captured, there are two primary pathways: geological storage (sequestration) and utilization.

Geological Storage

Captured CO₂ is compressed and injected deep underground into geological formations such as depleted oil and gas fields or deep saline aquifers. It is trapped through physical and geochemical mechanisms that can keep it securely stored for thousands of years.

Utilization

Captured CO₂ can be used in industrial processes, such as enhanced oil recovery (EOR), production of building materials, chemical synthesis of fuels and chemicals, and carbonation of mineral products. Long‑term climate benefits depend on ensuring that CO₂ remains stored rather than re‑released.

Global Status and Deployment

Carbon capture capacity has grown significantly in recent years, although it remains far from the scale needed to meet climate targets. As of the early 2020s, 31 commercial carbon capture facilities were operating globally, capturing millions of tonnes of CO₂ annually.

Total installed carbon capture capacity was estimated at over 40 million tonnes of CO₂ per year, with numerous projects in development worldwide. Countries such as the United States, Canada, Australia, Norway, and the United Kingdom have implemented policies and incentives to accelerate CCUS deployment. For example, the United States offers 45Q tax credits, which provide financial incentives per tonne of CO₂ captured and stored.

In addition to point‑source capture projects, several Direct Air Capture plants are under development or early operation, particularly in North America and Europe. These facilities, while currently small in scale (thousands rather than millions of tonnes per year), are crucial proof‑of‑concept steps toward larger DAC deployments.

Why Carbon Capture Matters for Climate Goals

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and major energy agencies have projected that even with aggressive deployment of renewables and energy efficiency, carbon capture will be needed to limit warming to 1.5 °C or 2 °C. In many scenarios, CCUS technologies cumulatively must capture gigatonnes of CO₂ annually by mid‑century.

This dual role — reducing emissions from hard‑to‑abate sectors and removing CO₂ already in the atmosphere — sets carbon capture apart as a key complement to renewable energy transitions.

Challenges and Limitations

Cost and Energy Requirements

Carbon capture technologies, particularly DAC, are currently expensive and energy‑intensive. The cost of capturing CO₂ can range from tens to hundreds of US dollars per tonne depending on the technology and application. Improving energy efficiency and reducing capture costs are central research priorities.

Infrastructure Needs

Successful deployment requires not only capture facilities but also CO₂ pipelines and permanent storage sites. Planning and permitting for large‑scale CO₂ transportation and storage infrastructure can be slow and complex.

Policy and Regulatory Frameworks

Long‑term policy certainty is necessary to attract investment in CCUS projects. This includes financial incentives, robust monitoring and verification standards, and clear regulations on storage liability and safety.

Public Acceptance

Local concerns about CO₂ storage safety and environmental justice issues must be addressed through transparent stakeholder engagement and rigorous safety monitoring.

Technological Innovation and Future Directions

Researchers and engineers are actively developing next‑generation carbon capture materials and systems, including advanced sorbents and solvents with lower energy regeneration requirements, membrane separation techniques, electrochemical methods, and integration with hydrogen production and renewable energy systems.

Growth in DAC technologies and hybrid systems that pair CCUS with bioenergy (known as BECCS) could provide scalable pathways to net‑negative emissions, an essential component of climate stabilization strategies.

Conclusion

Carbon capture represents a critical set of technologies in the portfolio of climate solutions. By enabling significant reductions in industrial and energy‑related CO₂ emissions, and offering the potential to remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere directly, CCUS supports both mitigation and long‑term climate resilience.

While challenges related to cost, infrastructure, and policy remain, global momentum is building through investment, innovation, and international climate commitments. As the world transitions toward net‑zero emissions, carbon capture technologies will play an indispensable role in shaping a low‑carbon future.
